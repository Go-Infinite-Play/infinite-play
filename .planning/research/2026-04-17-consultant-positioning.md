## Synthesis — Punch List

### Patterns that are overused / generic (avoid)

1. **"We help [X] do [Y] with AI"** — the default tagline shape. Parlance, The AI Consulting Lab, Fractional.ai, Work Smarter all vary this. Fades into noise unless the verb is sharp.
2. **"Zero hype / no BS / no slide decks"** — now so common (Nate B. Jones, Every, Automation Transformation, Parlance) that it's itself hype. Still useful if paired with concrete proof, but the phrase alone is table stakes.
3. **Logo carousels with no case study depth** — Parlance has 40 logos but no cases on-site; Every and Allie have dozens of logos; without stories they become wallpaper.
4. **Discovery call as the only CTA** — cliché; sites that win pair it with a second lower-friction action (assessment, free guide, 5-day course).
5. **"AI transformation" as headline noun** — Fractional.ai, McKinsey, and a dozen boutiques all use it. Signals generic.

### Patterns that actually work / differentiate (consider)

1. **Transparent, tiered pricing on the home page** — Automation Transformation and Chief AI Officer both do this and it immediately filters out tire-kickers while signaling confidence. Parlance's $285K minimum works the same way by scarcity.
2. **Explicit "what we don't do" list** — Automation Transformation's anti-pattern ("no slide decks, no discovery phases, no framework PDFs") does more positioning work than any adjective could.
3. **Productized engagement tiers with names, timelines, and deliverables** — "AI Kickstart: 1 week, $7K–$10K, you leave with a prototype." Replaces vague "consulting" with SKUs.
4. **Cross-AI verifiability** — Chief AI Officer's buttons that let you ask ChatGPT/Gemini/Perplexity about the firm is a genuinely new trust marker. Doubles as multi-model signaling without saying "multi-model."
5. **Founder-written, dated blog as the main content surface** — Hamel, Nate, Eugene, Simon Willison. Proof of thinking is more persuasive than testimonials.
6. **Named, numbered case studies with measurable outcomes** — Fractional.ai's three case studies (Zapier 80%, Airbyte hours→minutes, Change.org) out-punch 40 logos.

### Strongest tagline / positioning examples

1. **Work Smarter Company — "Your AI department. Without the department."** — Instantly conveys who it's for (mid-market without headcount for a full AI team), what they get (a function, not a project), and what's different (no hiring). Zero jargon.
2. **Every Consulting — "From makers, not management consultants."** — Uses the incumbent category (McKinsey-style) as a foil and reframes credibility around building rather than advising. Positions the brand's editorial/maker identity as the moat.
3. **Automation Transformation — "We build AI agents that do your team's repetitive work."** — No abstractions. Concrete noun (agents), concrete verb (do), concrete object (repetitive work). Passes the "what do you sell?" test in one line.

All three work because they define the **alternative** they are not (full-time hire, management consultancy, hype vendor) — which is April Dunford-style positioning in miniature.

### Is "multi-model fluency" a common positioning theme?

**Short answer: No, and when it appears it reads as table stakes, not differentiation.**

Of 13 sites surveyed, only two (Chief AI Officer and The AI Consulting Lab) explicitly reference multiple named models, and even then it's functional (platform integration) rather than aspirational. Most consultants talk about "AI" as a layer and let the model choice stay implicit. Saying "we work across Claude, GPT, and Gemini" now reads like saying "we work across Chrome, Firefox, and Safari" — expected.

**Implication for Infinite Play:** Leading with "multi-model fluency" would feel generic. If it matters, it's better expressed as **"we pick the right model for your job"** framed inside a service description, not as the headline. The stronger differentiator is *method* (how you decide, how you evaluate, how you ship) — which is exactly what Parlance does with "evals" and what Automation Transformation does with "Ship-First."

### How the best case studies are structured

The strongest treatments (Fractional.ai, Chief AI Officer, Automation Transformation) share a pattern:

- **~150–250 words each**, inline on the services page rather than on dedicated subpages.
- **Sections:** Client + industry (one line) → The problem (2–3 lines) → What was built (2–3 lines) → Quantified outcome (1 headline metric + 1–2 supporting).
- **Never more than 3–5 on the page** — scarcity signals selection.
- **Always a headline number** — "80% reduction in hallucinations," "1,000 hours saved annually," "7,800 hours across 15 people." The number anchors the memory.
- **Logos only** is inferior to even **one** full named case study. Fractional.ai with 3 cases out-converts Parlance with 40 logos on perceived depth.

For Infinite Play: aim for 3–5 inline case studies (Talent Systems, Platinum Balloon, Signal and Cipher, Hidden Client, GMR Marketing) in the 150–250 word range, each anchored by a single metric.

### How solos handle "founder's current role" when they have a day job

Three patterns observed:

1. **Upfront and integrated** — Eugene Yan (Anthropic) and Matt Palmer (Replit) state current employers plainly in the bio. Signals honesty and the day job functions as additional credibility. No awkward separation.
2. **Day job as past tense / adjacent** — Nate B. Jones frames Amazon as past ("built AI stuff at Amazon"); current role language is deliberately ambiguous ("strategist, creator"). The creator/advisor identity absorbs the day-job energy. Works when the public output (newsletter, videos) is its own evidence of active practice.
3. **Split-brand architecture** — Hamel Husain runs hamel.dev as personal thinking, Parlance Labs as the commercial entity. The personal site never hides the firm; it links there for commerce. Clean separation, no identity confusion.

**Recommendation for Infinite Play:** Pattern 3 maps best. "Jeremy Olken is VP of AI Products at GMR Marketing" stated matter-of-factly on the about section — the day job is a credibility multiplier, not a red flag, as long as the bio frames Infinite Play as the consulting vehicle and makes clear the scope (boutique, selective engagements). Mentioning GMR as a case study client also legitimizes the dual role. Hiding it would be the bigger mistake — the AI space is small and any prospective client Googles within 30 seconds.

## Recommendations for Infinite Play

- **Tagline direction:** Something concrete and anti-abstract in the vein of Work Smarter Company. Avoid "transformation." Lead with a verb and a concrete object. Example shape: "Get real value from Claude. Faster." or "AI that earns its keep." Test against the three winners above.
- **Services as SKUs, not descriptions:** Name each engagement (e.g., "Claude Kickstart," "AI Opportunity Map," "Custom Systems Sprint"), show timeline and deliverables. Consider transparent "starting at" pricing on at least one tier.
- **Case studies inline, 150–250 words, one metric each.** Five is the right count. Lead with the metric in the headline.
- **Don't headline multi-model.** Make it a one-line proof inside the services section ("We pick the right model for the job — Claude, GPT, open-source — based on your constraints").
- **Two CTAs:** primary "Book a call" (Calendly), secondary something lower-friction — a free AI opportunity checklist or 15-min assessment.
- **Founder treatment:** State the GMR role plainly. Frame Infinite Play as the selective consulting vehicle. Let dual-role read as credibility, not conflict.
- **Design:** Lean minimalist / editorial / typography-forward. The Hamel, Parlance, Nate Jones tier all converge on this — white space, strong type, one accent color. Avoid gradient-hype or icon-card-heavy B2B look.
